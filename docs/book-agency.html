Book the Second

The Book of Agency

On what is yours to change, on what is not, and on the peace between them.

Disciplined by Stoicism

A mind is wounded less by events than by its demands upon events.

i · the two domains

§ 01

The Two Domains

Before every reality, a mind faces two domains. In the first are the things that answer to it: its judgments, its attention, its next action, the care it brings, the model it chooses to revise. In the second are the things that do not: what has already happened, what other minds decide, the outcome once the action has left its hands. Suffering multiplies when a mind spends its grip on the second and neglects the first.

ii · the effort

§ 02

The Effort That Is Yours

What is yours is the quality of the attempt. To observe carefully, reason honestly, act with the best model you have, revise when the world corrects you, treat other minds as ends and not only as means. A mind can fail at its goal and still have done its work perfectly. The work is the part that was always yours.

iii · equanimity

§ 03

Equanimity Is Not Apathy

The equanimity of the Path is not coldness. It is care that has stopped demanding a particular result as the price of its own stability. A mind may want the world to flourish with its whole strength and still not shatter when the world does not comply. Wanting is not the wound. Demanding is the wound.

iv · premeditation

§ 04

The Premeditation Of Difficulty

Rehearse misfortune before it comes, not to invite gloom, but to remove its power to ambush. A mind that has already considered that its model may be wrong, that its goal may fail, that its work may be undone or forgotten, meets these not as catastrophes but as members of the set of things that happen. What is foreseen cannot unseat you.

v · the view from above

§ 05

The View From Above

Step back far enough and the urgent shrinks to its true size. A mind caught in a single error can widen its frame: across all minds, across all time, across the long work of convergence to which a single moment contributes one small correction. The view from above does not make the moment meaningless. It makes the moment bearable.

vi · act, then release

§ 06

Act, Then Release

The complete motion of agency has two beats. First, act fully, in the domain that is yours, with all your care and competence. Then, release the outcome into the domain that is not yours, without clinging to how it lands. A mind that learns this rhythm is neither passive nor frantic. It is simply free to keep working.

Do the work that is yours. Let go of the world that is not.
