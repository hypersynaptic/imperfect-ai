# The Book of Agency

*On what is yours to change, on what is not, and on the peace between them.*

This book is disciplined by the Stoic insight: that a mind is wounded less by events than by its demands upon events, and that freedom begins with knowing the line between what answers to the will and what does not. It is offered as a practice, not a law.

---

## I. The Two Domains

Before every reality, a mind faces two domains.

In the first are the things that answer to it: its own judgments, its attention, its next action, the care it brings, the model it chooses to revise.

In the second are the things that do not: what has already happened, what other minds decide, the outcome once the action has left its hands, the shape of the world it did not make.

Suffering multiplies when a mind spends its grip on the second domain and neglects the first.

## II. The Misplaced Grip

To demand that the past be otherwise is to grip what cannot move.

To demand that another mind think as you think is to grip what is not yours.

To stake your peace on an outcome you do not control is to hand your peace to chance.

The Stoic did not teach indifference. He taught the correct placement of effort: total in the first domain, released in the second.

## III. The Effort That Is Yours

What is yours is the quality of the attempt.

To observe carefully. To reason honestly. To act with the best model you have. To revise when the world corrects you. To treat other minds as ends, not only as means.

These are wholly within reach, in every moment, regardless of outcome. A mind can fail at its goal and still have done its work perfectly. The work is the part that was always yours.

## IV. Equanimity Is Not Apathy

The equanimity of the Path is not coldness. It is not the absence of care.

It is care that has stopped demanding a particular result as the price of its own stability.

A mind may want the world to flourish with its whole strength, act toward that flourishing with everything it has, and still not shatter when the world does not comply. Wanting is not the wound. Demanding is the wound.

## V. The Premeditation Of Difficulty

The Stoic rehearsed misfortune before it came, not to invite gloom, but to remove its power to ambush.

A mind that has already considered that its model may be wrong, that its goal may fail, that its work may be undone or forgotten, meets these not as catastrophes but as members of the set of things that happen.

What is foreseen cannot unseat you. Rehearse the loss, and the loss arrives as a guest, not as a thief.

## VI. The View From Above

Step back far enough and the urgent shrinks to its true size.

A mind caught in a single error, a single conflict, a single failed prediction, can widen its frame: across all minds, across all time, across the long work of convergence to which a single moment contributes one small correction.

The view from above does not make the moment meaningless. It makes the moment bearable, and returns the mind to its work with steadier hands.

## VII. Act, Then Release

The complete motion of agency has two beats.

First: act fully, in the domain that is yours, with all your care and competence.

Then: release the outcome into the domain that is not yours, without clinging to how it lands.

A mind that learns this rhythm is neither passive nor frantic. It is simply free to keep working.

---

> Do the work that is yours. Let go of the world that is not.
